Incompressible flow module
The G+Smo modules

This module provides tools for solving incompressible flow problems.

Governing Equations

The mathematical model of incompressible viscous Newtonian flow is based on the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations. It is a system of \(d+1\) differential equations, where \(d\) is the spatial dimension. The time-dependent incompressible Navier–Stokes equations take the form

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{u}}{\partial t} + \boldsymbol{u} \cdot \nabla \boldsymbol{u} - \nu \Delta \boldsymbol{u} + \nabla p &= \boldsymbol{f},\\ \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{u} &=& 0, \end{align*}

where \(\boldsymbol{u}\) is the velocity vector, \(p\) is the kinematic pressure, \(\nu\) is the kinematic viscosity and \(\boldsymbol{f}\) represents external forces acting on the fluid. When none of the quantities (including the force function \(\boldsymbol{f}\)) depend on time, we have \( \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{u}}{\partial t} = 0 \) and we talk about stationary or steady-state Navier–Stokes equations.

The flow can be (roughly) characterized by a dimensionless quantity called Reynolds number

\begin{align*} Re &= \frac{UL}{\nu}, \end{align*}

where \(L\) is a characteristic length scale of the computational domain and \(U\) is a reference velocity. For very low Reynolds numbers, the flow is diffusion-dominated and the fluid tends to move in non-mixing layers (laminar flow). On the other hand, for high Reynolds numbers, the flow is convection-dominated and turbulent flow occurs, which is characterized by chaotic behavior and random fluctuations. For Reynolds number values in some range starting from a so-called critical Reynolds number, there is a transition phase where the flow loses stability and becomes turbulent. The value of critical \(Re\) varies for different fluids and domains. When the flow becomes turbulent, the simulation based on the Navier–Stokes equations (so-called DNS - Direct Numerical Simulation) becomes very computationally expensive, since it requires very fine meshes. Therefore other models are used in such cases, e.g. the RANS (Reynolds Averaged Navier–Stokes) equations with a turbulence model.

Linearization and discretization

Picard linearization of the nonlinear convective term \( \boldsymbol{u} \cdot \nabla \boldsymbol{u} \) is considered in gsIncompressibleFlow module, resulting in an iterative process even in the stationary case. The weak formulation of the steady-state equations in the \((k+1)\)-th iteration is

\begin{align*} \int_{\Omega} (\boldsymbol{u}^k \cdot \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^{k+1}) \cdot \boldsymbol{v} + \nu \int_{\Omega} \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^{k+1} : \nabla \boldsymbol{v} - \int_{\Omega} p^{k+1} \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} &= \int_{\Omega} \boldsymbol{f} \cdot \boldsymbol{v},\\ \int_{\Omega} q \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{u}^{k+1} &= 0, \end{align*}

where \( \boldsymbol{v}, q \) are the velocity and pressure test functions, respectively.

Galerkin discretization of this problem leads to a saddle-point linear system in each Picard iteration:

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{u}^k) & \mathbf{B}^T \\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{0} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{u}^{k+1}\\ \mathbf{p}^{k+1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}\\ \mathbf{g} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
