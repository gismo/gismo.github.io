Manchester benchmark example

The Manchester contact benchmark (2006, 2008) uses the UIC60 rail profile and S1002 wheel profile.

ref. P. Shackleton and S.D. Iwnicki, "Comparison of wheel-rail contact codes for railway vehicle simulation: an introduction to the Manchester Contact Benchmark and initial results", Vehicle System Dynamics 46(1-2), 129-149, 2008.

  • The x-direction is in the rail forward direction.
  • The y-direction is the lateral direction. For the right rail of the track, negative y is to the track center and positive y to the field side.
  • The z-direction is the vertical direction, with the positive z-axis pointing downwards, into the rail.
  • The data are given in millimeters.
  • Points are numbered according to the right hand rule with the inside of the bodies on the right hand side when going through the points in increasing order.

The rail and wheel are each defined through a single cross section.

  • The rail is prismatic in x-direction. A 3D surface is created by selecting appropriate $x_i$ and forming the combinations $[x_i, y_j, z_j]$ for all available i and j.

  • The wheel is a body of revolution in x-direction. A 3D surface can be created by selecting appropriate $\theta_i \in [0,2\pi)$:

    $$r_j = 460 + z_j, ~ x_{ij} = r_j \sin(\theta_i), ~ y_{ij} = y_j, ~ z_{ij} = -460 + r_j \cos(\theta_i).$$

  • Another way to create a 3D surface is to define $x_i$ and invert $$x_i^2 + (z_{ij}+460)^2 = r_j^2, ~ z_{ij} = -460 + \sqrt{r_j^2 - x_i^2}$$
  • In these equations $z_j$ is the "principal profile" and $z_{ij}$ the 3D surface with nominal radius $r_{nom} = 460mm$.

The rail and wheel bodies are placed in space using coordinate transformation.

  • The rail is shifted down and to the right: $$O_r = [0, 760.5326, 7.3e-8], ~ X_{r(tr)} = O_r + X_{r(r)} $$ Here $X$ stands for the vector $[x,y,z]$. The suffix $ refers to the rail body coordinates, $ to the track coordinate system. The 'gauge width' between the two rails is $1435 mm$.
  • The wheel is rotated by roll angle $\phi_{ws} = -3.554e-4 rad$ (clockwise positive) and yaw $\psi_{ws} = 0.0120 rad$. 
    snr  = sin(roll); csr = cos(roll);
Rotx = [ 1,0,0; 0,csr,-snr; 0,snr,csr];
sny  = sin(yaw); csy = cos(yaw);
Rotz = [ csy,-sny,0; sny,csy,0; 0,0,1];
Rot  = Rotx * Rotz;
     = [ 0.9999, -0.0120,      0
         0.0120,  0.9999, 0.0004
        -0.0000, -0.0004, 1.0000 ]
    The wheel shift is $$O_w = [-8.9998, 755.1090, -0.2650], ~ X_{w(tr)} = O_w + Rot \cdot X_{w(w)}. $$

Two contact patches are found as shown in figure mbench_a22_5mm_rgt_tr.jpg. Their weighted centers are obtained as $X_{cp1(r)} = [-8.04, -23.76, 1.10], delta_{cp1(r)} = -0.133 rad$ and $X_{cp2(r)} = [-6.95, -33.93, 3.14], delta_{cp2(r)} = -0.298 rad$.

Grid-based contact search

The contact search is implemented in CONTACT in module m_wr_locatecp. We can ignore the quick scan (phase 1) and focus on the grid-based method.

  1. we need to define an interval for rail parameter $v_r$ that gives uni-valued $z_r$ as function of $y_r$. In CONTACT we have used trimming but now add a 'top view' administration for that. For the current example we may build the surface model for a trimmed surface to avoid this complication. Select j for which $z_j<10mm$. From the surface model, we get a range $[y_{min}, y_{max}]$ for the rail.
  2. we consider contact only at the bottom portion of the wheel, with $\theta$ between $-7deg$ and $+7deg$ (lowest point at $0deg$) with $\sin(7deg)$ about 0.125. We define $x_{min} = -r_{nom}/8, x_{max} = r_{nom}/8$ in wheel coordinates. A transformation to track coordinates then tells us which portion of the rail is of interest to the analysis.
  3. we define a 'gap mesh' on $[x_{min},x_{max}] \times [y_{min},y_{max}]$ in track coordinates with suitable step sizes $dx$ and $dy$, where $dx$ can be $0.5 mm$ or $1.0 mm$ and $dy$ is usually 2 to 5 times smaller to accomodate large slopes $\alpha$ in lateral direction.
  4. the rail surface $z^r_{ij}$ is evaluated at points $(x_i,y_j)$ of the gap mesh.
  5. the wheel surface $z^w_{ij}$ is obtained by determining a mesh in wheel coordinates, transformation to track coordinates, followed by interpolation.
  6. interpenetration areas are determined looking for $g_{ij} = z^r_{ij} - z^w_{ij} < 0$.
  7. the mid-point per interpenetration area is determined as $$x_{ref} = \int_{g_{ij}<0} g_{ij}^2 \cdot x ~ dxdy / \int g_{ij}^2 ~ dxdy,$$ $$y_{ref} = \int_{g_{ij}<0} g_{ij}^2 \cdot y ~ dxdy / \int g_{ij}^2 ~ dxdy,$$ $$z_{ref} = evalsurface(rail, x_{ref}, y_{ref}).$$ The average surface inclination per interpenetration area is determined as $$a_{ref} = \int_{g_{ij}<0} a_{ij}^2 \cdot x ~ dxdy / \int g_{ij}^2 ~ dxdy.$$ Here $a_{ij}$ is the angle of rail tangent vector $[0,t_y,t_z]$ with respect to the horizontal axis $[0,1,0]$, ignoring the x-direction.
  8. Rectangular 'potential contact areas' are defined for subsequent calculations with origin $O$ at $(x_{ref},y_{ref},z_{ref})$ and orientation $a_{ref}$. The size is rounded to multiples of user-requested dx, ds. One or more rows/colums are added on each side as a safety precaution.

Grid-based undeformed distance

  1. Each 'contact patch' (potential contact area) holds a uniform grid $(x_i,s_j)$ in the local coordinate system. We evaluate the surface heights $n^r_{ij}, n^w_{ij}$ of the rail and wheel in the local normal direction. 
     [nr] = eval_surface(rail, [x], [s], Rot)
 [nw] = eval_surface(wheel, [x], [s], Rot)
    The undeformed distance follows as $h_{ij} = n^r_{ij} - n^w_{ij}$. The velocities (vx,vy,vz) per point follow from prescribed motion of the rigid bodies evaluated at the surface positions.
